Sensitivity analysis for causal inference

Causal inference with observational data has mostly relied on the strong ignorability (unconfoundedness) assumption, which is unfortunately not testable and hard to justify in practice. I worked on a few projects about sensitivity analysis, aiming at understanding the robustness of causal conclusions against unmeasured confounding.

We connect sensitivity analysis with distributional shift: the conditional distributions of potential outcomes given measured covaraites in treated and control groups are within a distance from each other, and the distance is characterized by the sensitivity models. Thus, counterfactual inference under unmeasured confounding becomes a distributionally robust estimation problem.

In this paper Sensitivity analysis of individual treatment effects: a robust conformal inference approach, we work under the popular marginal sensitivity model to provide reliable prediction for counterfactuals. This tool infers the individual treatment effect (ITE) for each test sample, and could return a numeric value that indicates the strength of evidence for positive ITEs. An interesting observation is that the counterfactuals and the observables are within a bounded distribution shift.

In this paper Sensitivity analysis under the f-sensitivity models: a distributional robustness perspective, we extend the distributional robustness perspective and propose a novel class of sensitivity models. We allow the impact of unmeasured confounding to be bounded on average, instead of everywhere. This also makes the connection of sensitivity analysis and distributional robustness more clear.
